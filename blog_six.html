The bias blind spot is the tendency of people who see themselves as less susceptible to nonconscious predispositions and cognitive influences than others (Pronin, 2020). The Harvard test presented black faces, white faces, good words such as love, joy, peace, and bad words such as evil, hurt, failure. The challenge was to take this test at speed to see if users preferred white people over black. Psychologists have “critiqued the paradigm by saying it merely tests awareness of stereotypes, not endorsement of them, But the subconscious cannot distinguish between the two; it speaks only in the language of association” (Hutson, 2013). This test was interesting as it predicted the behaviour of users. Self-stereotyping was a key factor which played an important part as people didn’t “want to admit their biases to themselves (Hutson, 2013). Biases are stuck in our brain. “Rooted in the human brain’s automatic processing systems, unconscious decision-making has played an important role in the survival and evolution of species” (AESC, 2021). Many processes that we encounter on a daily basis can reflect our biases towards different situations. For example, if us as young users continued to hang out with people who don’t like older people, our brain influences this bias towards older people which is stuck with us in our cognitive memory until we overcome this problem.
